Human Welfare Research Framework

A research initiative exploring whether the ethical and stewardship principles preserved in the world's major wisdom traditions can be applied — rigorously and measurably — to the practical challenges of sustainable development.

Our central question is simple: in regions where conventional development approaches have struggled to deliver lasting outcomes, can interventions grounded in a community's own ethical heritage produce results that policy alone cannot? It is a question that has been asked in many forms over many decades. Our contribution is methodological: we are attempting to test it under the discipline of contemporary research standards, with verifiable data, transparent assumptions, and an explicit willingness to find no effect.

The framework is currently in pre-pilot stage. We have catalogued principles from ten traditions — Hindu, Islamic, Christian, Buddhist, Confucian, Jewish, Sikh, Taoist, Jain, and Bahá'í — mapped to specific United Nations Sustainable Development Goal indicators. We are designing a regional pilot in the Hoshiarpur district of Punjab, India, where the ecological conditions of the Shivalik foothills and the cultural inheritance of the region together present a setting where the framework can be honestly examined.

The work is not yet finished. We say so because the value of any future findings depends on what we choose to claim now. Until field measurements are taken, we hold our methodology open to scrutiny rather than our conclusions to defence.

What we are now

Current stage: Pre-pilot.

What exists today: A documented methodology, a verified facts register catalogued under a Zero-Exaggeration standard, a structured catalogue of cross-traditional principles, and an open design for a regional pilot in Hoshiarpur, Punjab.

What does not yet exist: Field data. Pilot results. Tested outcomes. We do not present a "Wisdom Dividend" figure because we have not yet measured one. We do not claim institutional partnerships beyond those that have been formally consolidated. We do not report findings from research that has not yet been conducted.

Why this matters: A research framework that promises results it has not earned is the most common form of credibility loss in this field. We have built the project's foundation around resisting that pattern. The methodology page describes how.

We welcome serious engagement from researchers, scholars of the wisdom traditions, sustainable-development practitioners, and institutional partners interested in the framework's regional or methodological dimensions. Contact details are on the contact page.
