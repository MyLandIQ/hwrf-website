Project Hoshiarpur

A research-led regional pilot

Project Hoshiarpur is the framework's proposed regional pilot. It is sited in the Hoshiarpur district of Punjab, India, where the ecological pressures of the Shivalik foothills and the cultural traditions of the region together create conditions under which our central hypothesis can be examined honestly.

The pilot is in design stage. It has not yet been launched. The description below explains why this site was chosen, what the pilot proposes to test, and the gates under which any future findings will be produced.

Why Hoshiarpur

Four characteristics of the district converge to make it the right site for a regional pilot.

Forest and biodiversity. Hoshiarpur holds the largest forest cover of any district in Punjab — 697.31 square kilometres, which is approximately 38% of the state's total forest cover concentrated in a single district. Roughly 32% of the district's own area is forested, against a state-wide average of 3.67%. The Lalwan Community Reserve in Hoshiarpur is among the first such reserves notified in India under the Wildlife Protection Act, providing a documented legal precedent for community-led stewardship within the very district being proposed.

Ecological stress. The Kandi belt within the district — a sub-mountainous zone at the base of the Shivalik Hills — is described in the published soil-conservation literature as part of "one of the eight most degraded eco-systems of the country." Average annual soil loss in the Shivalik foothills is approximately 16 tonnes per hectare per year, and in some watersheds exceeds 80 tonnes per hectare per year. Punjab's overall stage of groundwater extraction is 164%, against an annual extractable resource that current extraction has long since outstripped.

Latent regenerative capacity. Within the state-level stress, Hoshiarpur exhibits an intermediate profile. Long-term water-table decline in the Kandi zone is approximately 28 centimetres per year — slower than Central Punjab's 59 centimetres per year. Hoshiarpur's paddy water productivity, at 0.605 kilograms per cubic metre of irrigation water, is the highest among the five districts surveyed in the most recent published assessment. The district is stressed but has not yet reached the depth crisis observed in deeper-southern districts. A region at this threshold can still be stabilised.

Cultural context. The region preserves living traditions — Sikh, Hindu, and other — whose stewardship vocabularies are documented in primary scripture and historical record. The pilot is designed to examine whether interventions explicitly aligned with these existing ethical frameworks can reduce the institutional cost of achieving sustainable-development outcomes, compared with conventional approaches.

What the pilot proposes to test

The pilot is structured around two intervention clusters.

The Environmental Stewardship Cluster will test whether activating traditional community-led land-management practices and stewardship principles can lower the per-hectare cost of achieving soil-conservation and water-stewardship outcomes, compared with regulation-led approaches. Measurement will be of the deltas in soil retention rates and groundwater recharge, tracked through community-led reporting systems triangulated against independent measurement.

The Ethical Economics Cluster will test whether trust-based collective stewardship structures rooted in regional traditions can reduce supply-chain intermediation costs in the horticulture and beekeeping sectors of the district. Measurement will be of the fiscal impact on local producers, against control producers operating through conventional intermediation.

Both clusters are designed for a "Delta Auditor" methodology — the pilot is structurally ready to record a finding of no effect as readily as any other. The methodological commitment is to measure, not to confirm.

Verification gates

The pilot's progression is gated, following the framework's broader verification structure.

Gate 1 — Baseline Lock. Before any intervention activity, a Hoshiarpur-specific baseline is established for soil loss, runoff coefficient, groundwater behaviour, supply-chain cost, and participation indicators. Several measurements currently held as PENDING in the Verified Facts Register are sourced and locked at this gate.

Gate 2 — Field Data Milestone. After the pilot's first full quarter of validated field data, results are reviewed. This gate is also the trigger for any wider institutional engagement.

Gate 3 — Independent Audit. At project end, the dataset is reviewed by an independent fiscal and social audit. Effect, if any, is computed from observed deltas at this gate, not from any prior expectation.

Legal and institutional framework

The pilot operates entirely within existing legal and regulatory standards. No new legislation, special permissions, or extra-statutory authority is required. The Punjab Land Preservation Act, 1900, governs land conservation in the Shivalik / Kandi zone. The Wildlife Protection Act, 1972, as amended in 2002, provides the legal framework for community reserves. The Panchayati Raj framework provides the local-governance authority through which community-led land and water management can be formally engaged. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023, governs data captured in the course of the pilot, with participant data treated as belonging in the first instance to the communities that produced it.

The pilot is being prepared with the same care, and under the same Zero-Exaggeration discipline, that governs the wider framework. When findings emerge — including the possibility of no effect — they will be reported as the measurements show them, anchored to the Verified Facts Register, and not before.
